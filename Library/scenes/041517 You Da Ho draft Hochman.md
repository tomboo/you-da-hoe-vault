ONE

Old Girl wanted to kill herself in the worst way. Well, scratch that. She wanted to kill herself in the most appealing, least painful way, which was not that easy when all you had to go on were the life experiences of Hello Kitty.

Nine young adults--- "YAs," as the staff at Inner Horizons called them to differentiate them from the "ADOs" who were 17 and under and equally immature--- all straggled into the living room for Process Group. People dropped or flopped or lay curled up in fetal position on three couches, arranged in their usual off-kilter circle, before the meditation gong rang at ten AM.

Healing House's rust and gold tones and nubbly beige rugs and heavy oak looked Idaho-homey. Bland landscape paintings on the walls matched the bland, jmuted plaid on two couches. The oldest, most comfortable couch was the one people fought over. It was upholstered in a jumble of small black and gold letters that no one could read. It was a decor that reminded Old Girl of home in the worst way. It made her remember what used to be home while being a million miles away from it.

Darlene, the therapist on duty, staked out the sacred wingback chair. She called Process Group to order. And then introduced the newest member of the Inner Horizons team like a cheerleader -- "Meet Rachel Interlachen! Yay!"

On the weekend, Rachel had been frog-marched into Healing House by two ex-cops in matching ski jackets and T*rust in God, Buy a Gun* baseball caps. That meant Rachel's mom and dad had paid a bundle to have their flipped out, freaked out, raging mad, or catatonically comatose daughter escorted to Idaho by personal guard to get her into treatment.

Rachel, a tall girl with very dark skin and a hollow face, wore a short-sleeved tee shirt that said "Give Up the Ghost." On her eggplant-colored wrists, there were raised horizontal scars. Not vertical. Garden-variety self-injurer, Old Girl concluded, like most of the girls in the program, including herself.

Get prepared, newbie, they already took away your hair straightener (so you can't burn yourself) and your perfume (so you can't drink it) and the only knife you'll see will be in the hands of a bitch-faced cook who commits hate crimes against food.

Old Girl tuned out Darlene's introduction. She contemplated a three-foot long icicle spear on the eave from the ottoman she'd moved near the window. It had started forming several weeks ago. Now it was the largest fang in a mouthful of jagged teeth that lined the eave. If the icicle gave way, would it knock her brains out or impale her?

A curtain of snow cut off any hint of the massive Ponderosa pines that ringed the yard. It had started falling last October and was apparently going to keep on falling until May, Dim sulfurous light beat its way through the gloom.

Old Girl was eleven when she made her first suicide attempt. She had raided the medicine cabinet at her guardians' house in Naperville, Illinois. The problem was that Aunt Peggy and Uncle David were health nuts. Every multivitamin and mineral in the universe along with jumbo size bottles of Sam-E extract and glucosamine-chondroitin and chia seeds and flax seed caplets and fish oil jammed the shelves, but Ex-Lax was the only thing that had a "Keep Away from Children" warning. How was she supposed to know that shitting yourself to death was the world's most embarrassing way to go?

At 17, after a year and a half in Adolescents, Old Girl now applied strict rules to the list of acceptable ways to kill herself. Nothing gross, for example. Ditto, no vomiting. After the ignominy of being towed out of Lake Minnetonka by an Eagle Scout camp counselor who thought someone had pushed her beyond the ropes---picture three cracked ribs from chest compressions and lake water she expelled so hard that she burst a blood vessel in one eye ---all fluids were verboten. Double-ditto road rash, like the kind she got when she'd fallen out of Uncle David's Buick turning a corner on the way home from picking her up at middle school. Her body weight carried her out the unlatched door and she learned a good lesson in physics about objects in motion. It wasn't worth all the handwringing and recriminations, not to mention a broken arm and the harrowing experience of having gravel picked out of her hip with forceps.

Hanging? Definitely off the approved list. The big objection to asphyxiation was something she'd read about how your tongue swells up three times its normal size and then hangs out of your mouth like a brown banana. No thank you.

When Old Girl moved from Ads to YAs after New Years, she left her single room at Calm Cottage ---don't even get her started on the Program's stupid names---and moved into Healing House which was 15 miles from Couer d'Alene. The property bordered on the Kootenai National Forest. Next door on the other side was the dump.

She couldn't see any improvement in the level of maturity from Ads to YAs, unless she counted the fluency in psychobabble. The first thing new resident learned was to spout on like paid professionals about accountability and personal validation and developmental delays.

Healing House was also a lot more beat up than Calm Cottage. It was a one story ranch house. Old Girl assumed that was to prevent people from jumping. The living area separated two sleeping wings that angled away from each other. The wings were named for old Indian tribes: girls lived in the Flat Head; boys slept in the Black Feet wing. It took about one nanosecond for the wing names to devolve to the Fat Asses and the Black Heads. Somewhere in Dr. Hazey's small empire of repurposed residences around town, he had a manor home named for the Shoshones where the Show Ponies cavorted.

Naturally Fat Asses couldn't fraternize with Black Heads, and vice versa. Residents ate together, processed together, recreated together, and slept by themselves. God forbid you actually got close to anyone.

The wall thermometer outside read 28 degrees. An hour before, it was 23 degrees. Yippee! Idaho's definition of spring.

At breakfast, she'd overheard Margaret O'Leary, the one Day Supervisor she liked, yelling out the front door to the fix-it guy who picked up the trash whenever he felt like it. Margaret told him to get off his lazy ass and knock the icicles off the eaves before they killed somebody.

It hadn't occurred to Old Girl, who was consumed by thoughts of suicide, that you could simply stand under some hanging ice and let it put you out of your misery.

How would you know when an icicle was ready to drop? The insistence in Margaret's loud voice that morning made it seem imminent. "The temperature is rising," she'd told the trash man who drove a rackety ancient truck that held double stacked rows of jouncing barrels. Maybe melting was the key. The icicle dripped moisture when it warmed up during the day, then refroze into lethal weapons at night.

Thinking about gravity and physics gave Old Girl a headache---it was where she always got into trouble in her suicide attempts---so she switched to imagining what she would look like laid out in the snow like a fallen angel.

Instead of the gothic and practical dreads she'd been growing since Aunt Peggy and Uncle David left her in Idaho with vague admonitions to "get well," Old Girl would let her straight hair fan out like a sun storm in a corona around her head. She had Dad's blight of freckles but the felting and matting process of dreads had squeezed the copper out of her hair. Now it looked dull as cardboard. Death would pale her freckles; she supposed. Would it also flatten her hooked nose?

Usually she covered her strange proportions with baggy jeans or dark shapeless sweats. Her long legs made her look like a stork with the heavy breast of a turkey. The Fedora hat she wore to look hip wasn't very practical when it was freezing. She would have to carry it in her backpack and wear the black, pilling ski hat with the earflaps until the last minute.

Combing out her dreads out would be a terrible job.

Margaret would have to wield the scissors. Anything sharper than a butter knife was off limits at Inner Horizons. Too fucking many cutters.

Lydia Bronfman was a cutter. She had lost the argument about who had to process first. She was also a nympho and bulimic and the only child from some famous beer-making family in Michigan. You could hear the mid-west in her nasal singsong Accountability Letter for fucking Axel Friedman, her therapist. Everybody was mad at Lydia for getting Axel fired.

Lucky Axel.

This wasn't like school where they got rid of the troublemakers by expulsion. No way would Inner Horizons give up Lydia's \$12,000-a-month fees. It wasn't clear to Old Girl why Lydia was apologizing. None of her peers gave a shit who Lydia fucked. It would have been like counting her family's bottles of beer on the wall since she had sex with everybody. Only Big Paul, the Aspergery math wizard who sat alone in the corner, could count that high.

Lydia channeled Marilyn Monroe while she listed behaviors that included blowing and sucking and corn holing in a breathy voice. "Axel pushed my hand away when I touched his zipper," she said, enjoying her audience. "He said it wasn't appropriate but that's not what his woody was saying."

Darlene rapped her clipboard. "Skip the crap, Lydia. Tell us what you're taking ownership of."

Lydia blinked blue, ingenuous eyes accented by dime-sized sapphire earrings the size of dimes. "How can I take ownership if I don't tell you what I did?"

Darlene pinched the rutted space between her eyes. "Keep it moving."

"Well, he was moaning... you know? But in a good way...."

Old Girl had a hard time sleeping because her mind was too busy fabricating scenes of her death. She liked scenarios that were as plot driven as mysteries. The devil was in the details. Jimmy Choo shoes. Vera Wang dresses. Weather and setting, depending on her mood, varied between dark and creep-meister or grainy film noir or occasionally, like that poem she liked about Icarus falling into the sea, she would imagine a fishing boat that sailed on oblivious to a body falling out of the sky. Tragedy didn't have to slap you in the face.

She wondered how much warmer it had to get to make the icicle do its work. If she watched the temperature during Process Group three times a week and checked it before dinner every day, she'd get a read on the melting and refreezing progress. The problem with real life was that there was so little you could control.

"Angela! Wake up!" The edge in Darlene's voice, cut into her daydream. For a moment, it sounded like Mom calling her out of the pages of a book to come and set the table.

"Bring your ottoman back in the circle, Angela," Darlene said.

Old Girl shook her head. She absolutely, categorically, unequivocally would *not* answer to the name she'd been given before her family vanished. The day Aunt Peggy and Uncle David dumped her off in Idaho with two big suitcases and an ache in her throat from having her stomach pumped, she'd renamed herself Old Girl. If she could be thrust in the middle of some alternate existence, the new existence could at least bestow on her a new identity. Why couldn't she become Old Girl when everything that made her a child had been ripped away?

"We're waiting?" said Darlene.

"I don't know anybody named Angela."

"I'm sorry. Would Old Girl please join us?" Darlene put air quotes around the name "Old Girl."

The pleasures of double homicide-suicide were in her mind as she swiveled obediently back to the group. A black-and-white photo gallery took up one wall of the living room. Thirty or so people from the early 20^th^ Century were eyeing Process Group with judgmental eyes. Old Girl wondered what the old street sweeper at work with a hand-made broom or the brick maker who carried hundreds of pounds of bricks on his shoulder would make of the whining and rationalizing and griping and blaming and circle-jerking that defined processing.

On the bright side, she added the number 29 to the running tally she kept in her head of the number of feet underground that the old Angela was buried. Young Angela was never coming up to breathe.

Lydia was now blabbering about how she'd coerced a police officer with a blowjob. Her short black curls bobbed over a pink cashmere sweater. "Too bad it didn't work with his partner."

Darlene made a note, and then flipped to a clean page on the clipboard.

"It's obvious you're not really ready to take responsibility for your actions, Lydia. You and I will revisit your Accountability Letter in session."

So Darlene had inherited Lydia in the therapist's shake up after Axel's departure.

Old Girl was secretly glad she hadn't had to switch therapists when she moved to Young Adults. Dr. Hazey, the vague and disorganized founder Inner Horizons, had been her therapist from the get-go. A Yale degree in psychiatry on Hazey's wall said he specialized in Adolescents, probably because he still was one, though the man was practically senile. She'd seen the year 1986 on one of a dozen diplomas mounted on his office wall one day when she was too wound up to sit during session. That would mean Hazey had to be what... almost fifty?

James Olson, the blonde, quiet surfer from Malibu, California, now had the floor. He'd gotten caught with a bag of Jelly Bellies and a Milky Way in his backpack, which was small potatoes compared to the real reason he was in the Program. He'd crashed his birthday Porsche stoned on weed or poppers---"basically out of his mind" he'd said in one of those tense, shame-fests that made you want to run away from Process Group and climb in bed and never get up. He was saying, "I traded my Revos for the candy," when Darlene wanted to know how he could afford Jelly Bellies on his \$5-a-week snack allowance.

That was how the economy worked at Inner Horizons. A \$300 pair of sunglasses bought you \$5 worth of sugar high. Darlene wanted to know whom he traded with.

"Somebody from NICC."

Smooth, James. Old Girl knew for a fact that Reza, his room mate and a meth-addict motor-mouth, had supplied the candy because Reza's New York Jets cap had the Revos hanging off the back of it. Smart move on James part to put the blame on somebody at Northern Idaho Community College. After three months without running away, Young Adults were allowed to take 10 credits a quarter "under close supervision." If James had really sold the Revos at NICC, he would have gotten at least a hundred bucks with his trade. At Inner Horizons, all James had to do was tell Mommy or Daddy he lost his sunglasses and they'd send another pair Fed Ex. Ching-ching-ching. A galaxy of Milky Ways would show up in the stash under his mattress.

Out of the corner of Old Girl's eye, another drip of moisture beaded up and rolled down the icicle to the edge of the eaves. It hung there as if time in Idaho had the unique ability to stretch to its farthest dimensions. Time thinned until Old Girl blinked and then the droplet was gone as if it had never existed. That's how she liked to think of her own death. Moving from some place where you were stuck to a new, shiny state of existence where mothers and fathers and baby brothers didn't die at the mercy of a geriatric senior citizen who couldn't tell the gas pedal from the brake.

By the time seven more people processed their accountable behaviors, they had covered cocaine addiction, glue huffing, predatory sex and incest, shoplifting, theft, assault and battery, sibling torture, road rage, indecent exposure, arson, and vandalism --- if it hurt you or the people who loved you, the Inner Horizons family had tried it--- and then it was it newbie Rachel's turn. Darlene urged her to share something with the group. Inner Horizons was big on sharing. "Tell us about you," Darlene said. "What behaviors would you like to own up to?"

The curtain of streaming snow was now so thick it cut off any hint of the blue-black wall of fir trees that ringed the yard. The sun hung out there somewhere. Too weak and wan to do more than ooze sulfurous yellow light through the swirling flakes.

Old Girl felt for the newbie who glanced outside,then at the ceiling as if she was looking for an escape hatch. She wondered if all Rachel's cutting had aged her as much as Old Girl's failures had aged her. Rachel hunched her shoulders as if she was trying to protect her heart. She opened her mouth like a fish. Old Girl got the impression that there was not enough air in the room passing over her gills. She stood up, as she was going to address the group, but instead, she toppled slowly backwards, legs buckling at the knees, straight into James Olson's lap.

FIND A PLACE FOR

Time was maddeningly elastic at Inner Horizons. You were stuck in the never ending present with no ability to go forward and an 80% chance that you were going to go backwards if you fucked up. The clock ran forward for short periods but if you earned a demerit, say, for failing to take the trash out or fell into a depression and couldn't get out of bed, or you refused to participate in some stupid tell-the-truth game during Process Group like the one going on right now, the clock would turn around and run backward and taunting away from you. Old Girl had experienced heading toward a weekend recreational passes or a once, a chance for a weeklong trip home to Aunt Peggy and Uncle David, and just when she thought she was close to getting on a plane, the crazy clock would rewind and she'd find herself back at ground zero.

If she had known the clock was broken, she could have accepted it but the fact that the clock ran fast sometimes and slow at others and then took a god-awful leap backward to put her at zero over and over again made her hopeless with despair.

Two

HAZEY CALLED MARGARET INTO HIS office at 9 AM. Gretchen, the nutritionist, shot her a Grinchy smile. Shit in a shoe! Had the witch ratted Margaret out for saying the oatmeal sucked?

Margaret needed her crappy, low paying job at the "worst run, most dysfunctional psychiatric treatment facility West of the Mississippi." That's what Darlene, who'd been trying to land another job for months, had told a colleague that morning on the phone.

Margaret couldn't afford to leave, no matter how crappy the pay. Her husband, Hank, ruptured a disk moving a 300-pound patient at his job as a nurse at Benewah Health almost a year ago. They'd been living on one income ever since. The taxes on their 10-acre farm were due next month. She had no idea how she was going to pay them.

Hazey sat king-like behind his gleaming walnut desk. Not a paper on it. Only a folder and an expensive fountain pen the shape of a silver bullet.

\"Gretchen said you contravened her authority and questioned her personal worth in front of the residents.\" Hazey loved big words with chewy syllables. In fact, what Margaret had said to Gretchen Beener, possibly the only trained dietician in the world who couldn't follow a recipe for oatmeal, was, \"Are you trying to poison them? This shit tastes like charcoal."

\"The oatmeal was burned,\" Margaret said carefully. \"The kids were complaining that it tasted foul.\"

\"They\'re always complaining,\" he said. \"They\'re kids.\"

Well, Hazey had that right. Even though all the kids in the program had psychiatric issues, they were still, at heart, kids. Quick to laugh at Margaret's dumb jokes. Quick to whine and complain. Quick to point out the disappointing behavior of adults. Just like her own kids in their teenage years, just more fucked up.

\"I\'m a really good cook,\" Margaret said. "I could take over breakfast. Gretchen's always complaining she doesn\'t have enough time for meal planning.\" Really, she was always complaining that she needed a cigarette, which perhaps accounted for the fact that every taste bud on her tongue was dead.

Hazey frowned down at a glossy color brochure with a picture of a tropical blue ocean and palm trees for so long Margaret wondered if he\'d forgotten how to read.

She waited a decent amount of time. When he didn't respond, she asked, "Is everything OK?\"

\"I need a vacation. The stress is killing me.\"

Doctor Hazey didn\'t look stressed. He was reclined in a leather chair with his feet on the desk. At 49, the same age as Margaret, he looked like he took care of himself a lot better than she did. A tall tweedy man with gelled salt-and-pepper hair, he wore his usual professorial sport jacket with suede elbow patches and dress slacks. The paisleys in his tie looked like red eyeballs.

Margaret couldn't take her eyes off the shoes that were thrust in her field of vision. One was a black wingtip with a shiny, flesh-colored sole. The other shoe was brown. A wingtip, but scuffed on a worn black sole.

"Dr. Hazey, do you know your shoes don\'t match?\"

He slid the palm trees over a fraction of an inch and peered at his feet. They looked like two narrow barges on the lake of the desk. The news seemed to take a long time to reach his brain. \"I can\'t meet Lydia Bronfmann's mother in these.\"

Lydia's mom was making a fly-by? The only family that Margaret knew about was Lydia's paternal grandparents, the ones who made a fortune in liquor in Michigan. Margaret racked her brain and couldn't recall Lydia receiving any phone calls or letters.

"I thought her grandparents had custody?"

Hazey shook his head. Not a hair moved. "Her mother has custody but she has to see Lydia once a quarter or she loses it."

In Sandpoint, Idaho, where Margaret grew up, custody was when they took you in the back of a police car to the Cottonwood penitentiary.

"Lydia said she wouldn't talk to her." Hazey sighed. He flipped to a page of elderly sunbathers without wrinkles near a cerulean sea.

"Look on the bright side. She probably won't notice your shoes," Margaret said.

Whatever Hazey meant to tell her when he called her into his office got co-opted by the shoe crisis. Ten minutes later, Margaret found herself behind the wheel of Hazey's Lexus SUV. She was amazed to drive a car with heated leather seats. Only 1900 miles on the odometer.

It was easy to find Hazey's enormous glass and concrete house on the west shore of Lake Coeur D'Alene. It was the only aggressively contemporary place in a neighborhood of log houses that were built to look like cabins but were five times larger than the one Margaret had grown up in.

Hazey's driveway was clear of snow. Dry as a desert. Shit and Canola! He had a *heated* driveway?

Margaret's lower back winged at the sight of river-blue and rust-streaked slate, the only spot of color in a uniformly white neighborhood. She'd spent an hour that morning shoveling her ten-year-old Suburban out of the rutted country lane she and Hank lived on. Hank couldn't lift a bag of dog food since the accident.

Normally the fact that Hazey made more money than she and Hank would ever see in a lifetime didn't bother her. But was a 5% raise too much to ask?

Hazey was known to call Inner Horizons an albatross around his neck or when he was in a bad mood, a vampire that sucked his blood.

If Inner Horizon's was a vampire, quite a bit of blood had been sucked into building the glassed-in, two-story entry. Gleaming black wood floors. A red and black art rug with a zigzag pattern that was repeated in exactly the reverse order in the living room. Margaret's dream of having an electric garage door opener embarrassed her for its poverty of the imagination.

By the time she worked her way back to the master bedroom, passing not one but *two* white sofas that would have stayed clean in Margaret's house for less than five minutes, she was convinced Hazey truly was insane.

Some forty pairs of shoes were arranged in mahogany cubbyholes. Anybody could make the Hazey's mistake with so many pairs of shoes, she thought, except that the chandelier in the closet was big enough to light up Chicago. Who would put a chandelier with a million crystals where no one could see it? Margaret wondered for perhaps the thousandth time, had getting a Ph.D. replaced Dr. Hazey's common sense?

She was about to leave by the side door in the kitchen when she heard the clanging of garbage bins. Out front, she spotted Rusty Harrow, who picked up trash for all the Inner Horizon's facilities. Unincorporated Kootenai County was too backward to have its own garbage service.

Rusty was pawing deep in a garbage bin, occasionally holding up junk to his inspection. Probably collecting anything salvageable to sell at the dump he'd inherited from his old man. Margaret shrank back from the kitchen window so she wouldn't be seen.

There was something off about Rusty Harrow. Actually, there was something off about all the Harrows, starting with the old man, Stan Harrow, who started the dump back in the fifties. It was hidden on the other side of the butte from Healing House about a mile and a half as the crow flew, but five miles away by dirt road.

Stan Harrow beat the crap out of those kids. Three of the Harrow boys were older than Margaret and Rusty was in her grade through elementary school to whatever year he dropped out of high school. She still remembered him coming to school with occasional bruises and black eyes. Once when she sat behind him in the 8^th^ grade, a trail of dried blood ran out of his ear.

These days, you'd call CPS. Back then, everybody turned a blind eye. What people did at home was their business.

No wonder all of Rusty's brothers and the one sister---Olga? Helga? --got the hell out as soon as they could. All except Rusty who'd inexplicably stayed on. When Stan died in a mysterious trailer fire, Rusty took over the dump and made a living picking up from anybody willing to pay him under the table.

Now and then, she'd see him one of the six other facilities that comprised Hazey's psychiatric empire. He was still driving his old man's ancient stake-bed truck. Amazing the old junker still ran. She hadn't talked to him in years, other than to yell at him for leaving the bins overturned in the driveway. He looked like time had been hard on him. He'd always been built like a fireplug, but now his black, greasy hair was receding and he'd gone to flab in the gut. His shoulders were powerful and muscled from a lifetime of hefting trash barrels. Margaret still remembered the musty, feral smell that trailed him in the halls of high school and wondered if he was still allergic to bathing.

The smell of smoke wafted by Margaret's nose from the cat door. Damn. Now Rusty was taking a smoke break.

Margaret supposed Hazey had contracted with him to pick up trash here. Haley probably buried his personal fees in with the business bills. Just one more way bosses made money and people like Margaret didn't.

She wandered past a glossy black dining table with red leather chairs. A sad grouping of water-starved poinsettias ran down the center. Maybe it used to look festive but now, a month after Christmas, pale spindly arms reached weakly for the sun. Withered leaves dirtied the black veneer.

Where was Hazey's housekeeper? Margaret had counted nine Grape-nuts-encrusted cereal bowls in the sink. When she looked in the fridge, there was a single carton of 1% milk and four bottles of RÃ¶derer champagne. Margaret's Costco-stocked cupboards and jars of home canned fruit and a freezer half full of Sausage, the pig Hank had raised and butchered last fall, made her life look pretty full. Did the man eat out every meal except breakfast?

What a life.

By the time she wandered back to the master bathroom, she had to take a pee. She resolutely refused to look in the black low-slung toilet. Some things you just didn't want to know about your employer.

Clearly, the man didn't know how to pick up after himself. His bed was unmade. Dirty tee shirts and balled up underwear and a pair of pajama bottoms dotted the carpet. A certain dishevelment had cropped up in Hazey's dress after Alma's departure. She preferred thinking about her boss as an overspending, luxury-flaunting chucklehead than a lonely, slightly down-at-the-heels man unraveling at the seams like his pajamas. She opened the medicine cabinet to look for an Excedrin and did a double take.

Holy shit!

Hazey\'s cabinet wasn\'t just a medicine cabinet; it was the whole drug cornucopia that Inner Horizons kept stocked for its residents. Magically reconstituted in the director's bathroom! Her first thought was that Hazey must have been dying. HIV/AIDS? But Hazey seemed completely asexual. Uninterested in either sex. So that didn't fit.

She read labels for a prescription for Klonopin. Another for Atavan. Diazepam. Valium. Delaudid. Tirazepam. A bottle of Clonazepam prescribed to April Cohen whom Margaret remembered as a New Yorker with the investment banker dad who had moved his daughter to McLain when Harvard opened their new anorexia wing.

She reached for a bottle of Adderall; this one belonged to Reza Silverstein. Reza was the motor mouth ADHD kid from Camden, New Jersey, who couldn't sit still for love nor money. Hazey had gained weight after Alma's departure but a week or two ago, he'd been bragging about how he'd lost twenty pounds "just like that!" He punctuated how easy it was with a snap of his fingers. Just say no, he'd said. Was her boss saying No to food while he said Yes to Reza Silverstein's Adderall?

When Angela Livengood's name showed up on a bottle of Lorazepam, Margaret's maternal hackles went up.

Old Girl had a hell of a time sleeping. The kid was famous with the Night Supervisors as a card shark. It was common knowledge among the night staff if you needed help staying awake on your shift, all you had to do was walk by Old Girl's room with a heavy tread. She'd come right out with a deck of cards.

Margaret liked Old Girl. She was wicked funny. Never complained. Personally, she would have preferred that Old Girl would bitch and moan or even howl at the moon if it helped relieve her depression.

That morning, Old Girl had asked Margaret about the danger from falling icicles. "You're not thinking of standing under one are you?" Margaret had asked.

"What---you think I've got a hole in my head?"

"No, but I think you might want one."

"You never let me get away with anything!"

"Who would I have to bug if you weren't around?"

Old Girl had flipped one floppy deadlock away off her forehead and sighed. "Of all nut houses in the world, I get the one with Mother Fucking Teresa."

By the time Margaret read all the labels on the shelves, she counted up 89 prescriptions of a psychotropic nature, the majority for kids who had left the program.

About half way through her inventory, she registered the rumble of Rusty Harrow's truck leaving the driveway. Margaret\'s brain was busy solving the conundrum of the enormous drug stash in Dr. Hazey\'s home bathroom and she no longer had time to think about how crazy Rusty Harrow was.

Dr. Hazey was writing new prescriptions for whatever drugs he wanted in the names of kids he\'d treated and those records were available in Hazey\'s office. Margaret cleaned his office every week and she knew that the key to the locked file cabinet was kept in the top right drawer of his desk. Handy to Hazey and anybody else who wanted to know whatever he didn't want them to know but was too lazy to hide.

She wished she hadn't taken all the bottles off the crammed shelves. Damn. What if Hazey organized his drugs in a certain order? She randomly tucked them in wherever she found space. Nothing she could do about it if Hazey figured out that she\'d spent an interesting half hour perusing his personal Pleasure Chest.

She was so shaken by the discovery of Hazey's drug stash, she forgot the damn shoe she'd been sent for. She remembered it on the way out of his gated community. The one with the sign that said Kootenai Reserve. Reserved for everybody but the Kootenai Indians, they meant.

The shoe was where she left it on the kitchen counter. Size 13 Triple EEE Brooks Brothers with a hole wearing through the sole.

On her way out, the arms of the neglected poinsettias on the dining table seemed to reach out to her. She felt a wave of guilt. Honestly, she wasn't a jealous person. She was a good employee. One who cared about the Inner Horizons kids and wanted them not to suffer.

She emptied out the kitchen teapot and gave all the plants a drink. The image of lonely Dr. Hazey eating Grape-nuts at the kitchen sink in his wingtips sucked the green envy right out of her and she even loaded all the lonely bowls in the dishwasher.

Dr. Hazey. He was crazy as a loon, she supposed, but he was her loon. Now what in creation was she going to do about it? Leverage. She needed some goddamn leverage to get Hazey to do the right thing.
